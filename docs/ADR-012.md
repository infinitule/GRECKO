# ADR-012: PM — mutual co-evolution (Blue adapts back)

**Status:** Accepted
**Phase:** PM — mutual co-evolution

## Context

PILLAR C (PL) evolved Red attacks against a **fixed** Blue, and PX confirmed the
economic allocator's cost-exchange advantage. ADR-006 and ADR-010 both deferred
the natural next step — letting Blue adapt back to the discovered Red tactics —
until the PX evaluation loop existed to confirm whether adaptation actually
improves the cost figure. It now does, so PM closes the loop.

**Scope:** simulation only. A Blue "loadout" is a resource-allocation choice
over effector *parameter sets* (cost, kill probability); it is not a hardware
action, and choosing cheaper effectors is an economic decision in the sim.

## Decisions

### Blue policy = effector loadout + rationing knob

The adaptable Blue parameters (`league/blue.py`) are deliberately small and
interpretable:

- **loadout** — the effector type carried by each of the three interceptors,
  chosen from a cheap→expensive menu (collision $800, net $3k, kinetic $90k).
  `ew_soft_kill` is excluded: it is RF-only soft-kill with Pk=0 against
  quadrotors, useless against the kinetic probe.
- **lambda_cost** — the existing cost-rationing knob in [0, 0.5].

The loadout is the dominant cost lever, and the search space is small enough
(10 unordered loadouts × a handful of lambda values) to enumerate exhaustively
and deterministically.

### The loadout is injected, not hardcoded

`BridgeScenario` gained an optional `loadout` (and `magazine`) parameter,
mirroring the sensor-mesh / comms-config injection added for PS. Interceptor
*positions* stay fixed; only the effector type (and an arena-tuned speed)
varies. The cost comes from the effector catalogue, so changing the loadout
changes defense spend end to end with no other code path touched.

### Blue minimises cost-per-intercept, penalised for leakers

`blue_score = CER + 4·mean_leakers` (lower is better). The leak penalty stops
Blue from "winning" the cost metric by simply under-defending. In practice the
intercept rate is nearly constant across loadouts (see finding below), so the
leak term rarely discriminates between loadouts and the score is cost-driven —
which is the honest result, not a tuning artefact.

### The central finding: capability is not binding, cost is

The decisive measurement (constant Red, varying Blue loadout):

| Loadout         | Intercept rate | Cost-exchange ratio | Spend / episode |
|-----------------|----------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| all-kinetic     | 25%            | 90.0                | $255,000        |
| default (k,n,n) | 22%            | 32.0                | $80,000         |
| all-net         | 23%            | 3.0                 | $8,000          |
| all-collision   | 22%            | **0.8**             | **$2,000**      |

Intercept rate barely moves (~22–25%) because it is **interceptor-count
limited** — three consumable interceptors against an 8-UAS raid cap intercepts
at 3/8 regardless of effector — while cost swings **~100×**. Against cheap
commercial quadrotor swarms, an expensive kinetic interceptor is economically
irrational: every effector in the menu kills a quadrotor with similar Pk
(collision 0.70, net 0.78, kinetic 0.85), so the rational Blue fields the
cheapest one that still kills.

### Red cannot claw back the cost axis

When Red counter-evolves (a short ES against the adapted Blue), it can raise
leakers at the margin but **cannot make Blue's effectors more expensive** — the
cost axis is outside Red's action space. The adapted Blue's cost-exchange
advantage is therefore robust to Red counter-adaptation, which the arc confirms
(the clawback on CER is exactly 0). This is the one-sided-but-stable equilibrium
the mutual loop exposes: Blue's win is structural, not a fragile best-response.

The headline arc (league-seeded 3-tactic Red library, 30-policy Blue search):

| Blue                         | CER  | intercept rate | spend / ep |
|------------------------------|------|----------------|-----------|
| default (vs PL tactics)      | 34.4 | 25.8%          | $95,500   |
| **adapted (all-collision, λ=0.1)** | **0.8** | **25.8%**  | **$2,267** |
| adapted (vs counter-evolved Red)   | 0.8     | 28.3%     | $2,267    |

**97.7% lower cost-per-intercept (≈43×) at an identical intercept rate**, and
zero cost clawback after Red counter-evolves.

### Cost adaptation is free on the capability axis (PS gap)

Re-running the PS reality-gap study with the adapted loadout closes the
question PS left open. The cost-adapted Blue **preserves its intercept rate
across the reality envelope** — the platform-stable, count-limited metric — so
fielding cheaper effectors costs nothing in defensive capability. (The absolute
engagement-margin extremum is geometry- and BLAS-backend-sensitive and varies
across platforms, so the robust conclusion is rate-preservation, not a specific
margin number.) The remaining defensive gap is a *capacity* limit (three
interceptors vs eight threats), not an effector-cost limit — recovering it needs
more interceptors, consistent with the count-limited finding above.

> **Determinism note.** Replay determinism holds *within* a platform (same seed
> → identical event-log hash, gated in CI). Continuous geometry metrics such as
> the min-intercept margin can differ *across* BLAS backends; the project's
> headline claims (cost-exchange ratio, intercept rate) are integer-grounded and
> platform-stable, and the test suite asserts on those rather than on exact
> margins.

### One-sided exhaustive Blue search, not co-evolutionary ES for Blue

Blue's space is small and discrete, so an exhaustive deterministic search beats
a stochastic optimiser: it is reproducible, needs no population tuning, and
returns the global optimum within the menu. Red keeps the (μ+λ)-ES from PL. The
asymmetry matches the problem — Red's space is a 14-D continuum, Blue's is a
handful of discrete loadouts.

## Rejected alternatives

- **Co-evolving Blue with an ES too:** unnecessary for a 10-element discrete
  space; exhaustive search is both optimal and deterministic. Rejected.
- **Magazine-mix as the Blue lever:** with three consumable interceptors and a
  small raid, magazine depth is never the binding constraint, so a mix knob has
  no measurable effect. The loadout (per-interceptor effector) is the real
  lever. Rejected in favour of loadout.
- **Including ew_soft_kill in the menu:** Pk=0 vs quadrotors; it would only ever
  add dominated options to the search. Rejected.
- **Letting interceptor count vary:** that would change the intercept-rate
  ceiling and confound the cost finding; holding count fixed isolates the cost
  lever cleanly. Deferred to a future capacity-planning study.

## Acceptance evidence

- 19/19 tests pass in `sim/tests/test_pm_coevolution.py` (297 cumulative, 1 skip).
- The injected loadout changes interceptor effectors and defense spend end to
  end (verified): all-collision costs strictly less than all-kinetic, with a
  strictly lower CER when both intercept.
- `optimize_blue` returns the minimum-`blue_score` policy in the enumerated
  space (verified), and never selects the all-kinetic loadout against the cheap
  swarm.
- The full arc reduces (or holds) Blue's cost-exchange ratio vs the default
  Blue, and reports Red's clawback after one counter-evolution round.
- `BridgeScenario` loadout injection leaves the C2 interlock and determinism
  invariants intact (`tools/verify_invariants` still green).
