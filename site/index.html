Two decisions legacy doctrine never makes

Neither is magic. Both are economics — exactly what a saturating swarm is built to punish you for getting wrong.

Ration the magazine

Nine rounds against eleven threats — something leaks. Legacy fires at whatever's closest and runs dry, often leaking the one expensive target it never reached. GRECKO spends its nine on the nine worst and lets the two cheapest go.

Rank by dollar damage

Not by distance — by what it costs you if it lands. A $9,400 loitering munition outranks a $1,200 quad even when the quad is closer. The ranking is the whole game, and it updates every frame as tracks resolve.

Match effector to threat

A $90k kinetic round on a $1,200 drone is a losing trade even on a hit. When a cheap $800 soft-kill will do, GRECKO uses it — and reserves the expensive rounds for the targets that actually warrant them.

The name · why a gecko

Named for an animal that survives by giving something up

A cornered gecko drops its tail — it sacrifices a cheap, regrowable part to keep the whole animal alive. That is the exact move at the core of GRECKO: deliberately let the cheapest threats through to save the asset. The survival instinct is the algorithm.

  • Drops its tail to live

    Autotomy: shed the cheap part, save the animal. GRECKO's HOLD FIRE on the two lowest-value drones is the same instinct — the amber markers in the sim are the tail it lets go.

  • Sees the whole field in the dark

    A gecko's low-light eyes track everything at once. The sensing layer holds the full air picture — every drone valued and ranked before a round is committed.

  • Grips with no wasted force

    Van der Waals toe-pads hold with exactly enough, never more. Effector matching is the same economy — an $800 soft-kill where it suffices, a $90k round only where it's earned.

  • Spends energy only on the kill

    An ambush predator waits, then strikes once. GRECKO holds rounds in reserve rather than reacting to the nearest motion — the magazine is patience, not reflex.

Beat 3 · and the swarm adapts

A static defense eventually gets solved

The allocator above is real and runnable today. The reason GRECKO is built as a research platform — not a fixed rulebook — is that a competent adversary feints. The next frontier is a defense that co-evolves against one.

⚑ Roadmap — research frontier, not yet shipped
  • Economic allocator built · runnable
    Value ranking, magazine rationing, effector substitution — the engine driving the console above. Validated across seeds in the Python evaluation harness.
  • Intent prediction next
    Distinguish a feint from the main axis before committing rounds. A wave that splits is testing you; spending your magazine on the decoy is exactly the trap.
  • Adversarial self-play next
    A red-team that evolves swarm tactics the defense was never scripted against, and a tactic library that updates in response. Static rules lose to this; a co-evolving policy does not.

Six modules, one decision

No black boxes. Every allocation is logged and replayable, and the simulation-only scope is enforced mechanically on every commit.

ModuleCommandWhat it does
sim/coregrecko evalDeterministic battlefield at 50 Hz — tracks, guidance, hit resolution. Same seed, same outcome, every time.
sim/allocgrecko evalThe allocator from the console above: value ranking, concurrency cap, hold-fire, effector matching.
sim/bridgegrecko serveWebSocket C2 bridge streaming engagement state — the human-on-the-loop operator console connects here.
eval/grecko eval --seeds NMonte-Carlo runner: cost-exchange ratio, intercept rate, damage prevented, across randomized raids.
tools/verifygrecko verifyFour architectural invariant checks — runs on every CI commit, enforces the simulation-only boundary.
grecko/cligrecko --helpUnified entrypoint for every subcommand. Prints the scope statement on each version call.
