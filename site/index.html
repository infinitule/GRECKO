⚠ Scope

Simulation, research, and C2-software only. No fire-control, no RF/jamming/waveform design, no weapon or effector hardware control. Effectors are parameter sets (cost, kill probability). A CI gate enforces this boundary on every commit.

The problem — you can't win by spending

A $90,000 interceptor shooting a $1,000 quadrotor is a losing trade — even when it hits. Swarms weaponize that math: send enough cheap drones and any kinetic-first defense runs out of money before it runs out of targets.

Counter-swarm is not primarily a targeting problem. It is a cost-exchange problem.

GRECKO treats the engagement as an economic decision — which interceptor, against which threat, at what cost, under whose authorization — and optimizes the dollar exchange directly.

The moat — three compounding advantages

Economic allocator Pillar A

A magazine-constrained sequential optimizer that can hold fire on a low-value track to preserve rounds for what matters. ~22% lower cost-per-kill than greedy before loadout adaptation; ~98% after.

Adversarial co-evolution Pillar C

An in-house red team that evolves attacks the defense was never scripted against, then the defense adapts back. The cost advantage is robust: the adversary cannot make our effectors more expensive.

Auditable human-on-the-loop C2

Engagement is reachable only through an authorization interlock, proven by static analysis to have a single gated path. Every decision is logged and replayable — the prerequisite for certification and rules-of-engagement.
