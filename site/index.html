// 02 — the allocation problem

Why the legacy approach loses

Counter-swarm defense fails on economics, not on aiming. GRECKO fixes two decisions legacy doctrine never makes.

01
Magazine limit
You have 9 interceptors and 11 incoming drones. Something leaks. Legacy fires at the closest threat and runs dry — often missing the most expensive target it never reached.
02
Value ranking
GRECKO ranks every tracked drone by estimated asset damage value. It reserves 9 rounds for the 9 most dangerous threats and deliberately lets the two cheapest through.
03
Effector match
A $90k kinetic round on a $1,200 quadrotor is a losing trade. When a low-value threat can be stopped by an $800 effector, GRECKO substitutes it — and saves the kinetic rounds for targets that need them.
// 03 — what's built

Six modules, one decision

Every part of the engine is independently runnable. No black boxes — every allocation is logged and replayable.

Module CLI command What it does
sim/core grecko eval Deterministic battlefield simulation at 50 Hz — drone tracks, interceptor guidance, hit resolution
sim/alloc grecko eval Magazine-constrained economic allocator: value ranking, MAXCONC=4 concurrent cap, hold-fire logic
sim/bridge grecko serve WebSocket C2 bridge streaming engagement state at 50 Hz — operator console connects here
eval/ grecko eval --seeds N Monte Carlo runner: cost-exchange ratio, intercept rate, per-scenario JSON output
tools/verify grecko verify 4 architectural invariant checks — runs on every CI commit, enforces simulation-only scope
grecko/cli grecko --help Unified CLI entrypoint for all subcommands. Prints scope statement on every version call.

SCOPE GRECKO is simulation, research, and C2-software only. Effectors are parameter sets — no fire-control, no RF/jamming design, no weapon hardware control. This boundary is enforced mechanically on every commit by grecko verify.
